Color Theory Explained

Color Theory is the study of how colors interact, mix and influence one another. It helps artists and designers understand how to use color to create harmony,
balance, and emotion in their work. By learning color theory, we can make visual choices that grab attention and communicate meaning effectively.

Key Color Theory Terms

Primary Colors
Pure colors that cannot be created by mixing other colors. Examples include red, blue, and yellow.
Secondary Colors
Colors created by mixing two primary colors. Examples include green, orange, and purple.
Tertiary Colors
Colors made by mixing a primary color with a secondary color. Examples include red-orange and blue-green.
Complementary Colors
Colors that are opposite to each other on the color wheel.
Analogous Colors
Colors that are next to each other on the color wheel.
Triadic Colors
A group of three colors that are evenly spaced on the color wheel.
Split Colors
A variation of colors that uses one base color and the two colors on either side of its complement.
Saturation
The intensity or purity of a color.
Hue
The purest form of a color.
Tint
A hue mixed with white.
Shade
A hue mixed with black.
Value
The lightness or darkness of a color.
Tone
A hue mixed with gray.
Warm Colors
Colors that evoke warmth, such as red, orange, and yellow.
Cool Colors
Colors that evoke coolness, such as blue, green, and purple.
Color Wheel
A diagram that shows the relationships between colors.
Monochromatic Colors
Colors that are variations of one hue, including its tints and shades.
Contrasting
The impact of combining different hues in a way that highlights their differences.
Vibrancy
The intensity and brightness of a hue.
RGB
A color mode that uses red, green, and blue light in digital displays.
CMYK
A color mode that uses cyan, magenta, yellow, and black in printing.
